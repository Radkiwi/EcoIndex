Why It Matters

One action. Three layers of health.

Restoring native ecosystems isn't just an environmental act. The science is clear: it improves human health, land productivity, and ocean ecosystems — simultaneously.

🧬

Personal Health

Urban native canopy reduces heat stress, airborne pollutants, and childhood asthma. Research confirms that regular exposure to native biodiversity strengthens immune function and measurably reduces anxiety and depression in young people.

When students restore their neighbourhood, they restore their own biology.

Urban tree canopy can reduce local temperatures by up to 8°C and cut respiratory illness rates significantly
🌍

Land Health

Below the 15% native cover threshold, species loss in a catchment accelerates dramatically — and the cost to manage and restore it multiplies. Native root systems stabilise hillsides, rebuild soil microbiome networks, and restore the underground fungi highways that make agriculture sustainable long-term.

Dropping below 15% native cover per ecosystem type triggers rapid, compounding biodiversity loss — Eco-index's core finding
🌊

Ocean Health

Every catchment in Aotearoa drains to the sea. Riparian planting within 30m of waterways reduces nitrate and sediment runoff by 40–70% — directly improving the water quality of streams, rivers, harbours, and ultimately the ocean. What students plant on a hillside shows up at the beach within years.

Riparian buffers can reduce nitrate runoff by 40–70%, with measurable downstream impact reaching coastal waters
